The Singles Map: it's when, not where

The famous map sizes each metro by its surplus of single men (blue) or single women (red), ages 20–64. But that single number hides the real pattern — the imbalance is driven far more by age than by place. Below: the national age curve first, then the map you can scrub through age by age and watch flip.

The whole country flips around 40

National single men minus single women, by age, summed across all 229 metros. Young singles skew male almost everywhere; older singles skew female almost everywhere. The crossover sits at roughly 40–44.

The same flip, on the map

Drag the slider or press play to move through age bands. Watch the map turn from blue to red — the geography barely changes the story, the age does. Four points are cities, not metros (dashed outline): Los Angeles, Long Beach and Anaheim are split out of their shared metro, and New York City stands alone — hover any of them for the geography caveat.

Race / origin

Census 1-year tables break marital status down by race or by age, not both — so race views cover all single adults 15+ and the age slider is paused. Differences between groups partly reflect their age structure (older groups carry more widowed and divorced singles).
