The Singles Map: it's when, not where

The famous map sizes each metro by its surplus of single men (blue) or single women (red), ages 20–64. But that single number hides the real pattern — the imbalance is driven far more by age than by place. That correction is chapter one: below it, a matchmaking view measures your market, where who you are and what you seek matter more than either when or where.

Abstract The 2007 National Geographic "Singles Map" advised singles to move wherever their target sex is in surplus. Rebuilding it on __ACSYEAR__ Census data — published tables plus person-level microdata — we find that advice fails twice. First, the aggregate imbalance is set by age, not place: nearly every metro tilts male among singles in their twenties and female past 45, flipping around 40. Second, the same-age sex ratio mismeasures any individual's market, because partnering is cross-age and assortative. From 70 million observed couples we measure who actually pairs with whom by age and race, and compute each seeker's market — rivals per reciprocal match — for every metro, with margins of error from the Census replicate weights. The levers of a single person's market rank: (1) who you are — a 30-year-old man faces ~165 rivals per 100 reciprocal matches nationally, a 30-year-old woman ~128; (2) what you seek — income and cross-race filters move markets by multiples; (3) when you are in life — the age flip; (4) where you live — typically ±10–15% around the national benchmark, with "where" climbing the ranks as the search narrows. The map below is the argument, interactive: every claim can be scrubbed, filtered, and checked against its uncertainty.

§2 · The same flip, on the map — and your own market

Claim: where you live nudges the number; who you are and what you seek set it. Race / origin

Now modeled from Census PUMS microdata, so race groups are mutually exclusive (Hispanic of any race, then non-Hispanic White/Black/Asian/Two+) and — unlike before — respect the age slider. Race uses the 5-year __ACS5RANGE__ sample (not the 1-year), which pools ~5× the respondents: a race×metro×age cell is thin, and 1-year estimates for it are dominated by sampling noise — the Census Bureau's own guidance is to use the 5-year for small subgroups. (The overall and economic views stay on the latest 1-year __ACSYEAR__.) Race and the economic filter can't be combined (separate microdata cross-tabs), so choosing one resets the other. New York and Los Angeles appear as whole metros in these views; only the five Connecticut metros lack a PUMS estimate.

Econ / education

Modeled from Census PUMS microdata (__ACSYEAR__ 1-year), not the published tables: single adults 20–64 by personal income (total income in __ACSYEAR__ dollars), employment, or education (bachelor's degree or higher vs not). All of these respect the age slider. The education split is the Date-onomics finding made scrubbable: among singles 25–34 the ratio flips between the two pools — roughly 90 college men per 100 college women, but ~133 per 100 in the non-degree pool — so the "surplus of single men" and the "shortage of educated men" are both true, in different markets. These filters and race can't be combined (separate microdata cross-tabs), so choosing one resets the other. New York and Los Angeles appear here as whole metros (their city insets step aside); only the five Connecticut metros, which lack a clean PUMS-to-metro mapping, have no PUMS estimate.

The asymmetry, in one exhibit

Run the matchmaking benchmark at every age — national single counts, each age's default (middle-50%) search range — and the sexes trace opposite arcs. A man's market only tightens through his 30s, peaking near 190 rivals per 100 reciprocal matches around 40 — because he keeps reaching into a younger, scarcer, more-contested pool — before easing to ~94 by 60; a woman's is toughest young-to-midlife (~120–145 through her 30s and 40s) and never spikes the way his does. The curves don't cross until the late 40s: the male squeeze runs deep into midlife. The same-age sex ratio of §1 is structurally unable to see this — it is the age-gap kernel of §4, applied to the singles pool, that produces it. (The kernel is built from recently-formed unions, so an older seeker's odds reflect who pairs at that age today, not decades-old marriages.)

§3 · Where you live matters more the narrower your search

Claim: geography is a secondary lever — until the search narrows to a group concentrated in a few metros. The race filter in §2 doesn't just recolor the map; it changes how much the map can help you at all.

Each bar below spans the single sex ratio (men per 100 women, ages 25–34) from the 10th- to the 90th-percentile metro, weighted by where singles of that group actually live so a handful of tiny metros can't distort it — and each race band is drawn from the 5-year __ACS5RANGE__ PUMS, whose ~5× sample is what makes a race×metro cell reliable enough to rank at all. Search across everyone and the range is only ~23 points (104 to 127): moving to a top-decile metro shifts your ratio by roughly ±10% — real, but the secondary lever this thesis claims it is. Narrow to a same-race partner, though, and it depends entirely on which. White and Hispanic singles live nearly everywhere, so their range is no wider than the all-comers search — Hispanic is actually tighter. But the range for a Black partner runs about a third wider — and skews female (median 92 men per 100 women) — while an Asian partner's is roughly double, skewing male (median 121), because those pools concentrate in a few metros whose balance swings hard. That is the abstract's "where climbs the ranks as the search narrows," quantified — and quantified honestly: even on the 5-year sample the raw best-versus-worst metros look several times more extreme still, but that is small-sample noise these population-weighted bands strip out.
