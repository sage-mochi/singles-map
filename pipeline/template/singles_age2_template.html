The Singles Map: it's when, not where

The famous map sizes each metro by its surplus of single men (blue) or single women (red), ages 20–64. But that single number hides the real pattern — the imbalance is driven far more by age than by place. Below: the national age curve first, then the map you can scrub through age by age and watch flip.

The whole country flips around 40

National single men minus single women, by age, summed across all 229 metros. Young singles skew male almost everywhere; older singles skew female almost everywhere. The crossover sits at roughly 40–44.

The same flip, on the map

Drag the slider or press play to move through age bands. Watch the map turn from blue to red — the geography barely changes the story, the age does. Four points are cities, not metros (dashed outline): Los Angeles, Long Beach and Anaheim are split out of their shared metro, and New York City stands alone — hover any of them for the geography caveat.

Race / origin

Now modeled from Census PUMS microdata (2024 1-year), so race groups are mutually exclusive (Hispanic of any race, then non-Hispanic White/Black/Asian/Two+) and — unlike before — respect the age slider. Race and the economic filter can't be combined (separate microdata cross-tabs), so choosing one resets the other. New York and Los Angeles appear as whole metros in these views; only the five Connecticut metros lack a PUMS estimate.

Economic filter

Modeled from Census PUMS microdata (2024 1-year), not the published tables: single adults 20–64 by personal income (total income in 2024 dollars) or employment. Unlike the race view, these respect the age slider. Income/employment and race can't be combined — the microdata cross-tabs are separate — so choosing one resets the other. Here New York and Los Angeles appear as whole metros (their city insets step aside); only the five Connecticut metros, which lack a clean PUMS-to-metro mapping, have no PUMS estimate.
